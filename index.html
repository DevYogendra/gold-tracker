How To Read This

A quick theory of the chart

Commodity prices can swing hard in the short run, but over longer stretches they usually drift back toward production cost. This tracker uses price divided by cost to show whether today’s market is near its economic anchor or trading at a large premium to it.

Short Term

Demand shocks, ETF flows, central-bank buying, geopolitics, and inventory tightness can move price much faster than supply can respond.

Long Term

Cost acts like gravity. If price stays far above cost, producers usually add supply. If price falls near or below cost, weaker supply gets cut and the market often rebalances upward.

Reading The Ratio

A high ratio means the commodity is expensive relative to its production base. A low ratio means price is closer to its cost floor, which often tells you more about cycle position than price alone.

Use With Context

Gold can stay rich when monetary demand dominates. Silver and oil are usually more sensitive to industrial and energy balances. The most useful comparison is live ratio versus historical median.

Notes
Gold & Silver production costs = AISC (All-In Sustaining Cost). Oil "production cost" = avg breakeven cost per barrel. Gold data: annual averages from 1900 (US fixed price / London free market from 1968). Silver: annual from 1970. WTI & Brent: annual from 1970/1987 respectively. Sources: Kitco, WGC, Silver Institute, EIA/BP Statistical Review. Live metal prices via api.gold-api.com (free, no key). Live oil prices via EIA Open Data API (free, requires registration). All prices in USD. Markets closed on weekends — last close shown. Median and Mean ratios are time-weighted by year-span where data points are non-annual; with the current annual dataset all points carry equal weight.
